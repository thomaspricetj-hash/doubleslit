\# Information‑Compression Model of Wave–Particle Behavior  

\### A Computational Framework for Continuous Collapse and Re‑Coherence  

Author: Thomas Price (2026)



\## Abstract

This work introduces an information‑compression interpretation of quantum

behavior in which wave and particle characteristics arise from continuous

changes in an information‑state parameter c ∈ \[0, 1]. Measurement is modeled as

forced compression, producing localized Gaussian packets, while erasure

corresponds to re‑decompression. A numerical engine is implemented to simulate

double‑slit propagation, collapse, and re‑coherence. The model reproduces key

quantum phenomena and yields experimentally testable predictions, including a

linear visibility–compression relationship, detector‑geometry‑dependent packet

shapes, hysteresis under compression history, and timing‑dependent

re‑decompression effects.



\## Introduction

Standard quantum mechanics describes wave–particle duality but does not provide

a mechanistic explanation for collapse or re‑coherence. This work proposes a

unified framework in which quantum behavior emerges from transitions between

compressed and decompressed information states. The model is computationally

implemented and compared against known experimental behaviors.



\## Theory

A quantum entity is represented by ψ(x, y). Its behavior is governed by an

information‑state parameter c. Wave-like behavior corresponds to c = 0, while

particle-like behavior corresponds to c = 1. Measurement is implemented as a

compression event using a localized kernel. Removing information reverses the

compression, allowing the wavefunction to expand and recover interference.



\## Methods

The engine evolves ψ(x, y) using discrete propagation. Compression is applied

via Gaussian kernels. Visibility is computed from intensity profiles. Multiple

protocols test hysteresis, timing effects, and detector geometry.



\## Results

Simulations show:

\- V(c) ≈ 1 − c across the full range of c.

\- Collapse produces directional Gaussian packets.

\- Re‑decompression restores interference proportionally.

\- Compression history affects final visibility.

\- Delayed re‑decompression reduces recoverable coherence.



\## Discussion

The model provides a mechanistic explanation for collapse and re‑coherence,

naturally incorporates partial information, and predicts behaviors not captured

by standard interpretations. Several predictions are experimentally testable

using modern quantum‑optical setups.



\## Conclusion

The information‑compression model offers a unified, continuous description of

wave–particle behavior. Its numerical implementation produces clear,

quantitative predictions suitable for laboratory testing. This framework may

provide new insight into the role of information in quantum mechanics.
